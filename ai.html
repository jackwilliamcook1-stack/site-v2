Working with AI

AI assembles. I decide.

Everyone's resume says "AI tools" now. Here is what that actually means in my work, at three levels: building products with it, running real workflows on it, and knowing where it breaks.

1. Building products on it

Pest OS is an AI product with paying customers, not a demo. Voice agents answer real inbound calls for real operators: 97 calls handled in our first customer's first month, six jobs booked to calendar without a human touching the phone. Building it meant learning what production AI requires that prototypes don't: spam screening, graceful escalation to a human, and owning the failure when the agent mishandles a call. 2. Running real workflows on it

At GHC Partners I built an outreach pipeline that parsed family office PDFs, scored fund fit, and drafted personalized emails at scale, with human approval gating every send. The design principle: automate drafting, never judgment.

The part most people skip: verification

Midway through one build, the personalization script was producing clean, confident, single-sentence intros. I asked a simple question: are these actually correct? They weren't. The model had been inventing investment focus areas that sounded plausible but weren't in the source documents. So I scrapped the automated focus lines and manually verified all 87 companies against their source PDFs, in batches, until every claim traced to a document. Final count: 28 companies with verifiable focus areas, 59 left deliberately blank, and a do-not-contact list flagged in the CRM.

That instinct, that fluent output is not the same as correct output, is the most useful thing I know about AI.

Scale

Catch
Hallucinated focus areas, caught by manual audit
Fix
87 companies verified by hand against sources

3. Knowing where it pays, and where it doesn't

In a timed three-hour case for an enterprise AI company, I scoped agent deployments for two Fortune 500 workflows: distributor pricing exceptions at an industrial manufacturer, and SKU substitution at a food distributor. The exercise was less about AI and more about deployment judgment: sizing the revenue at risk (with assumptions stated and the data requests to replace them), mapping where the human stays in the loop, picking the wedge use case by adoption friction rather than by ceiling, and scoping a 90-day pilot with explicit "not attempted" lines like no ERP writes and no auto-approvals.

The lesson generalizes: the constraint on enterprise AI is rarely the model. It's data readiness, trust, and whether the people in the workflow want the thing to succeed.
