I build things, then study why they work.

I'm finishing my economics degree at Dartmouth this June. I co-founded Pest OS, a company that manages the entire customer lifecycle for pest and wildlife control operators — from the first missed call to the booked job. As a venture analyst at GHC Partners, I built the fund's LP outreach infrastructure from scratch: three proprietary databases covering 100+ potential LPs across the US and Northern Europe, automated pipeline tooling for ~700 family office contacts, and macroeconomic research that's been included in LP reports twice. I like building systems that work and studying the economics of why they do.

On the clock

I'm drawn to work where you have to build the thing and understand why it works — not just one or the other. At Pest OS, that means selling software to small business owners who have never bought software before, then watching the unit economics play out call by call. At GHC Partners, it meant constructing outreach systems for a venture fund while simultaneously researching whether the asset class even outperforms public markets. (The answer is complicated. I wrote a paper about it.)

Before all of that, I won first place in three pitch competitions at Tuck's entrepreneurship lab, bought an ownership stake in a small bed company to learn operations with real money on the line, and led an activist-investor case study on Callaway Golf that was graded 100%.

Off the clock

I fish more than I should admit on a portfolio site — brook trout in New Hampshire, stripers when I can get to the coast. I play lead guitar and harmonica in the Cherry Hills Band, which plays the kind of music that's better with a beer in your hand. I teach skiing at the Dartmouth Skiway and run the Dartmouth Billiards Club as president, which mostly means I'm the one who buys the chalk.

I speak Italian and lived in Rome for a term. That's where my co-founder Jacob and I decided to start a company about pest control, of all things — a conversation that started as a joke about the pigeons in Trastevere and turned into an actual business plan by the time we flew home.

I climb when I can, ski when I should be studying, and generally believe that the best way to learn something is to put money or reputation on it and see what happens.
